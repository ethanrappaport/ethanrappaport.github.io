About

I'm a mathematical biologist interested in how ecological and evolutionary dynamics shape microbial communities. My work bridges theoretical modeling and computational analysis, with a focus on colonization resistance, consumer-resource dynamics, and host-phage coevolution.

I completed my B.S. in Mathematics and Biology at Boston College, where I worked in the Momeni Lab. I'm now a PhD student in the BEES program at the University of Maryland.

Teaching

My teaching centers on making mathematics legible — not just procedurally, but conceptually. I am especially interested in how geometric intuition, phase portraits, and careful notation can make ideas from analysis and dynamical systems accessible to students encountering them for the first time.

Private Mathematics Tutor

Independent · 2025–present

One-on-one tutoring for undergraduate and secondary students in calculus, linear algebra, differential equations, and proof-based mathematics. Sessions focus on building conceptual fluency alongside computational skill — working through why a theorem is true, not only how to apply it.

Tutor and Grader

Department of Mathematics, Boston College · 2022–2025

Staffed drop-in hours at the Math Learning Center, supporting students across introductory calculus, multivariable calculus, linear algebra, probability, and proof-writing. Working across courses and preparation levels required quickly diagnosing where a student's understanding broke down and offering multiple framings of the same idea until one connected.

Math Instructor — Tricky Topics in Geometry

Summer Scholars Program, Friends' Central School · 2021

Designed and delivered a preparatory geometry course from scratch, covering triangle similarity and congruence, area and volume, and introductory trigonometry. Instruction was asynchronous via recorded lectures supplemented by daily live sessions — a format that demands unusually clean exposition, since every ambiguity a live instructor might catch in real time must instead be anticipated in the material itself.

Teaching philosophy

Mathematics is not primarily a collection of techniques — it is a way of seeing. In my teaching I lead with pictures and intuition, introduce definitions only once we have something concrete to define, and treat a student saying "now I actually see why that's true" as the real goal. I find that most barriers to mathematical understanding are about confidence and prior exposure, not ability.

I am particularly interested in helping students from biology and other sciences develop comfort with formal mathematical reasoning — drawing on my experience building models that had to be legible simultaneously to microbiologists, mathematicians, and physicists.
